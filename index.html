More Medieval Instruments

There are many more Medieval instruments that Adam did not have time to talk about in the podcast. Among them is the gemshorn, which was a flute made out of the horn of an ox. It was used in early Hebrew ceremonies. The vielle was similar to a violin but had a deeper and longer body. In addition, it had three to five strings. The pipe and the tabor were usually played by the same person, the pipe being played by one hand and the tabor being played by the other. The pipe was similar to a recorder but only had three holes. The tabor was a type of drum. As well as the lute and the harp, the harpsichord and the bagpipe were other familiar instruments used in Medieval times.

Medieval Style

Part of the unique Medieval style comes from the performances. Of course, the instruments played a large part in the sound of Medieval pieces. Medieval pieces were played by small groups, as Adam said, and this contributed to the tavern-like feeling of the music. Instuments accompanied voices at unison or octave for decoration. Polyphony was in wide use, with each instrument playing a different melody, in contrast to the modern style of homophony, a melody over an accompaniment. Parallel fifths and unconventional harmony were other important parts of the Medieval style. Many pieces were in the Dorian mode, as opposed to the common Ionian mode used in pieces today. However, an important thing to keep in mind is that there is a difference between the music of the Medieval and Renaissance periods, which are often associated.

More on the history of Medieval music

After the ars antiqua period of 13th century France came the ars nova period of 14th century France, "New Art." There was a flourishing of polyphonic music during this time, as well as developments in rhythmic notation. It should be noted that the Italian ars nova is different than that of the French. The Italian ars nova is another term for Italian trecento (1300) music, the period of flourishing for Italian music, which extends from the 1300s to 1420. It was largely brought about by the beginnings of troubadours, or people who played secular music for a living. After the ars nova came ars subtilior, "Subtle Art." The music of the ars subtilior style was rhythmically and notationally complex and was hard to sing. They had very subtle nuances, in direct contrast to ars nova music. The music of the Renaissance came about with the rise of humanism, the view of humans as the center of the universe, with the polyphony of ars nova and the subtleness of ars subtilior carrying on into future centuries.
